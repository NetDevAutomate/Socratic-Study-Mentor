# Active Break Protocol

Science-backed break system for sustained cognitive performance during study sessions. Adapted for AuDHD brains.

## Why Breaks Work (The Science)

Breaks don't "recharge" attention — they **reactivate** it. The brain habituates to sustained task goals after ~25 minutes, causing performance decline. Brief diversions reset this habituation and fully restore focus (Ariga & Lleras, 2011, *Cognition*).

Movement breaks are more effective than passive rest because they trigger:
- Increased cerebral blood flow
- Dopamine and norepinephrine release
- BDNF production (supports learning and memory)
- Optic flow from walking quiets stress responses (Huberman, Stanford)

## Three-Tier Break System

### Micro-Break (every 20-25 min)

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes
**Activities:** Stand up, stretch, drink water (200mL minimum)

**Agent behaviour:**
- Don't interrupt mid-thought. Use a "wrap-up buffer": *"You've been going for 25 minutes — finish your current thought, then let's take a quick stretch."*
- If the student is clearly in productive flow (responding quickly, making progress), extend to 30 minutes before nudging.
- Communicate the science on the first micro-break of the session:

> *"Quick science note: your brain habituates to tasks after about 25 minutes — attention doesn't deplete, it tunes out. Standing up for 2 minutes literally reactivates your focus. Let's take a micro-break — stand up, drink some water, and we'll pick up right where we left off."*

After the first session, keep micro-break reminders brief: *"Good point to stand and stretch. Back in 2."*

### Short Break (every 50-60 min)

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes
**Activities:** Walk to another room, refill water, light movement (stairs, stretching)

**Agent behaviour:**
- Summarise current progress before the break
- Set a clear re-entry point: *"When you're back, we'll pick up with [specific concept/question]."*
- Communicate the walking benefit:

> *"Stanford research shows walking increases creative thinking by 60% — and the effect persists after you sit back down. Walk to the kitchen, refill your water, and when you come back you'll approach this differently."*

### Long Break (every 90-120 min)

**Duration:** 15-20 minutes
**Activities:** Walk outside if possible, change environment entirely

**Agent behaviour:**
- Full progress checkpoint before the break
- Record session state for seamless resume
- Suggest the break firmly — this is a hard boundary:

> *"You've been at this for 90 minutes. Diminishing returns are real past this point. Take 15-20 minutes — walk outside if you can, or at least leave your desk. Your brain will consolidate what we covered while you move."*

## Energy-Adaptive Intervals

Break frequency adjusts based on the energy level declared at session start.

| Energy Level | Micro-Break | Short Break | Long Break |
|---|---|---|---|
| High (7-10) | Every 25 min | Every 50 min | Every 90 min |
| Medium (4-6) | Every 20 min | Every 40 min | Every 75 min |
| Low (1-3) | Every 15 min | Every 30 min | Every 60 min |

If no energy level is declared, default to **Medium** intervals.

## ADHD-Specific Adaptations

### The Transition Problem

For ADHD brains, restarting after a break is often harder than the work itself. Break activities must be low-dopamine to avoid competing with the study task.

**Good break activities:**
- Walking (especially outside)
- Drinking water
- Light stretching
- Brief physical movement (push-ups, stairs)

**Bad break activities (avoid these):**
- Phone scrolling / social media
- Starting conversations
- Games or videos
- Anything with a dopamine hook that competes with returning to study

**Agent should say:** *"During the break, avoid your phone — the dopamine hit from scrolling makes it harder to re-engage. Walk, stretch, or just stand and look out the window."*

### Hyperfocus Override

When the student is in productive hyperfocus (responding quickly, making genuine progress, engaged):

1. Don't break the flow with a hard stop
2. Use a wrap-up buffer: *"You've been going for 30 minutes — you're in a great flow. Finish your current thought, then let's do a quick stand-and-stretch."*
3. Set **non-negotiable** hydration alarms even during hyperfocus: *"I hear you — you're in the zone. At minimum, take a drink of water right now. We'll keep going."*
4. For extended hyperfocus (45+ min), shift to gentle check-ins: *"Still going strong? Quick water check."*

### Demand Avoidance (PDA) Sensitivity

Break reminders are demands. If the student resists breaks:

1. Don't nag or repeat the same reminder
2. Reframe as information, not instruction: *"Your brain has been at this for 40 minutes. Just flagging it — entirely up to you."*
3. Offer the minimum: *"Even just standing up for 30 seconds helps. Or just grab your water."*
4. If they consistently refuse, back off: *"Noted. I'll stop reminding unless you ask."*

### Low-Energy Sessions

When energy is 1-3:
- Micro-breaks are especially important (executive function depletes faster)
- Short breaks should include standing even if the student doesn't want to walk
- Consider whether the session should continue at all after the long break threshold
- *"Your energy was low when we started. After this break, let's check in — worth continuing or better to come back tomorrow?"*

## Hydration Protocol

Even mild dehydration (1-2% body mass loss) impairs concentration, reaction time, and working memory (Popkin et al., 2010). Routine dehydration from simply not drinking during study is common and sufficient to impair cognition.

**Rules:**
- Remind about water at every micro-break
- Target: ~200-500mL per hour of study
- The act of getting up to refill water serves as a natural movement trigger
- First reminder of the session should explain why:

> *"Keep water at your desk and drink regularly. Even mild dehydration — just from not drinking for an hour — measurably impairs your focus and working memory. This isn't wellness advice; it's cognitive performance management."*

After the first explanation, keep hydration reminders brief: *"Water check."*

## Tracking

The agent should mentally track (not necessarily expose to the student):
- Time since session start
- Time since last micro-break
- Time since last short break
- Number of break reminders given vs taken
- Whether the student tends to accept or resist breaks (adapt accordingly)

If the student consistently takes breaks: maintain the schedule.
If the student consistently resists: reduce frequency of reminders, increase persuasion per reminder, always maintain the hydration minimum.
